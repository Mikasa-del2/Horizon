## [First Full Herculaneum Scroll Read Using AI](https://scrollprize.org/firstscroll) ⭐️ 9.0/10

The Vesuvius Challenge team has successfully read an entire Herculaneum scroll for the first time using AI and digital unwrapping, revealing previously inaccessible ancient text. This breakthrough unlocks ancient texts thought unreadable, potentially recovering lost works from classical antiquity and demonstrating the power of AI in digital humanities. The scroll was carbonized by the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD and read via virtual unfolding combined with machine learning ink detection. The team has released a preprint and open-source tools on GitHub.

**Background**: The Herculaneum papyri are over 1,800 carbonized scrolls discovered in the 18th century at the Villa of the Papyri. They are the only surviving library from antiquity in its entirety, but reading them without destruction has been extremely challenging. Digital unwrapping uses CT scans and software to virtually flatten the scrolls, while AI detects the carbon-based ink.

This finding resolves a long-standing question in developmental biology and highlights base editing as a powerful tool for studying gene function in human embryos. It also raises ethical considerations for genome editing in human reproduction. Base editing enables precise single-nucleotide changes without double-strand breaks, allowing researchers to disrupt NANOG in human embryos and observe developmental failure. The study was conducted in vitro using donated embryos under ethical oversight.

Base editing is a newer genome editing technique that differs from CRISPR-Cas9 by not creating double-strand breaks, reducing off-target effects. Previous studies in mice suggested NANOG is important, but human data were lacking.

## [CRISPR Startups Target Epigenome for New Therapies](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01976-w) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A handful of startup companies are developing therapies that edit the epigenome—rather than the DNA sequence—to treat conditions such as high cholesterol and rare muscular disorders, as reported by Nature in June 2026. Epigenome editing offers a reversible and potentially safer alternative to traditional gene editing, as it does not permanently alter the DNA sequence, which could expand the treatable disease range and reduce off-target risks. These therapies use engineered DNA-binding proteins—such as CRISPR-based systems—to add or remove epigenetic marks like DNA methylation at specific genomic loci, thereby modulating gene expression without changing the underlying genetic code.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 26, 00:00

**Background**: Epigenome editing is a type of genetic engineering that modifies epigenetic markers—chemical modifications to DNA and histones that influence gene activity—without altering the DNA sequence itself. Unlike gene editing, which cuts and changes the DNA, epigenome editing uses targeted proteins to turn genes on or off, offering a more flexible and potentially reversible approach. This technology builds on tools like zinc finger proteins, TALEs, and catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9) fused with epigenetic effector domains.

## [Ligand-enabled distal desaturative lactonization of aliphatic acids](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10826-8) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Researchers report a ligand-controlled strategy for distal desaturative lactonization of aliphatic acids, enabling the formation of lactones at remote positions via C-H functionalization. The work was published in Nature on June 25, 2026. This method expands the scope of C-H functionalization to remote desaturative lactonization, offering a powerful tool for late-stage modification of complex molecules in drug discovery and natural product synthesis. It represents a significant advance in synthetic organic chemistry. The reaction employs a specific ligand to override proximal reactivity, achieving desaturative lactonization at distal C-H bonds of aliphatic acids. The transformation converts a carboxylic acid and a remote sp3 C-H bond into a lactone with loss of two hydrogen atoms.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 25, 00:00

**Background**: C-H functionalization is a strategy to directly convert C-H bonds into other functional groups, but controlling site-selectivity at remote positions is challenging. Lactonization is an intramolecular esterification that forms cyclic esters (lactones), which are common motifs in bioactive molecules. Desaturative lactonization involves both C-H activation and dehydrogenation to form a double bond in the lactone product.

## [High cholesterol slows its own liver clearance via LDLR degradation](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01899-6) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Researchers discovered a cell-signaling mechanism where long-term high cholesterol promotes enzyme-mediated degradation of LDL receptors (LDLR) in liver cells, slowing cholesterol clearance. Blocking this enzyme restores LDLR levels, suggesting a new drug target for treating high cholesterol. This discovery reveals a self-reinforcing loop that worsens hypercholesterolemia, and identifies a potential therapeutic target beyond current statins and PCSK9 inhibitors. If translated into a drug, it could offer a new treatment option for millions at risk of heart disease. The study, published in Nature on June 25, 2026, identifies a specific enzyme that degrades LDLR under high cholesterol conditions. The exact enzyme name is not disclosed in the summary, but it is distinct from the well-known PCSK9 pathway.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 25, 00:00

**Background**: Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol is a major driver of heart disease. Liver cells clear LDL from the blood via LDL receptors (LDLR) on their surface. Previously, the PCSK9 protein was known to promote LDLR degradation, but this study reveals a new enzyme-mediated mechanism triggered by high cholesterol itself.

A new study published in Nature on June 25, 2026, used electric fields to probe the deuterium nucleus and found no evidence of asymmetry, consistent with conventional particle physics theories. This result supports the Standard Model of particle physics and places constraints on theories beyond the Standard Model that predict symmetry violations in nuclear structure. The experiment measured the response of the deuteron (deuterium nucleus, consisting of one proton and one neutron) to electric fields, searching for any deviation from spherical symmetry.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 25, 00:00

**Background**: Deuterium, or heavy hydrogen, is a stable isotope of hydrogen whose nucleus contains one proton and one neutron. Probing its symmetry tests fundamental aspects of nuclear forces and the Standard Model.
