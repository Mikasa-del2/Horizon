## [Expansion microscopy boosts resolution to pinpoint amino acids](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01842-9) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers have developed a method that stretches protein samples isotropically by up to a billion times in volume, enabling individual amino acids to be resolved using standard light microscopes. This breakthrough was published in Nature on June 23, 2026. This technique dramatically simplifies super-resolution imaging, making it accessible to any lab with a standard light microscope. It could revolutionize structural biology by allowing routine visualization of proteins at the amino-acid level without expensive specialized equipment. The method builds on expansion microscopy (ExM), which embeds samples in a swellable polymer and expands them. The billion-fold volume increase corresponds to a linear expansion factor of about 1000x, far exceeding previous ExM limits of ~16x.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 23, 00:00

**Background**: Expansion microscopy (ExM) is a sample preparation technique that physically enlarges biological specimens by embedding them in a polymer hydrogel that swells upon hydration. This allows nanoscale structures to be imaged with conventional microscopes. Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) achieves super-resolution by isolating individual fluorescent molecules, but typically requires specialized setups. The new method combines extreme expansion with standard light microscopy to achieve similar resolution without complex optics.

## [New radical cross-coupling method for C-glycoside synthesis](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10807-x) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A team led by Phil Baran at Scripps Research has developed a novel radical cross-coupling reaction using glycohydrazides to efficiently synthesize C-glycosides, published in Nature on June 22, 2026. C-glycosides are important in medicinal chemistry due to their metabolic stability, but their synthesis has been challenging; this new method provides a general and efficient approach that could accelerate drug discovery. The reaction is redox-neutral and proceeds via radical cross-coupling of glycohydrazides with various coupling partners, enabling access to a wide range of C-glycosides with high stereoselectivity.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 22, 00:00

**Background**: Glycosides are molecules where a sugar is bonded to another group via a glycosidic bond. C-glycosides have a carbon-carbon bond instead of the more common oxygen linkage, making them resistant to enzymatic hydrolysis and thus attractive as drug candidates. Traditional methods for C-glycoside synthesis often require harsh conditions or multiple steps.

A new stereoretentive decarbonylative cross-coupling reaction enables direct C(sp3)-C(sp3) bond formation with retention of stereochemistry, published in Nature on June 22, 2026. This breakthrough provides a straightforward approach to stereocontrolled C(sp3)-C(sp3) bond formation, which is highly valuable for pharmaceutical and materials chemistry where precise stereochemistry is critical. The reaction uses chiral alkylnickel intermediates that are stable enough at low temperature (22-40 °C) to enable stereoretentive cross-electrophile coupling with alkyl radicals derived from alkyl iodides.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 22, 00:00

**Background**: Cross-coupling reactions are widely used to form carbon-carbon bonds, but controlling stereochemistry in C(sp3)-C(sp3) bond formation has been challenging. Decarbonylative cross-coupling involves extrusion of carbon monoxide from a carbonyl compound, offering an alternative to traditional halide-based couplings. This work builds on nickel-catalyzed decarbonylative strategies and addresses the stereochemical challenge.

## [First nuclear clocks tick, promising precision revolution](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01909-7) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Two independent research teams have built the first operational nuclear clocks, marking a long-awaited breakthrough in timekeeping technology. These clocks use a nuclear transition in thorium-229 instead of atomic electron transitions to keep time. Nuclear clocks could surpass the accuracy of today's best atomic clocks by orders of magnitude, enabling new tests of fundamental physics such as variations in fundamental constants. They may also improve GPS, deep-space navigation, and gravitational wave detection. The nuclear transition used is in thorium-229m, which has an unusually low excitation energy in the ultraviolet range, making it accessible with current laser technology. The clocks achieve a fractional frequency uncertainty below 1×10^{-19}, surpassing optical atomic clocks.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 22, 00:00

**Background**: Conventional atomic clocks rely on electron transitions in atoms like cesium or strontium, which are sensitive to external electromagnetic fields. Nuclear clocks use transitions within the atomic nucleus, which is better shielded from environmental disturbances, potentially offering greater stability and accuracy. The concept was first proposed in 2003, but technical challenges delayed realization until now.

## [Unlimited OCR: One-Shot Long-Horizon Parsing](https://github.com/baidu/Unlimited-OCR) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Baidu released Unlimited OCR, a one-shot long-horizon parsing method that uses a recurrent sliding-window attention (R-SWA) mechanism to keep the KV cache size constant, enabling transcription of hundreds of document pages in a single pass without memory growth. This approach eliminates the need for page splitting and reduces output latency, making long-document OCR practical for real-world applications like digitizing books or processing lengthy reports. The KV cache remains fixed throughout the process, so output latency stays constant regardless of document length. The model is open-source on GitHub and builds on Deepseek-OCR and PaddleOCR.

hackernews · ingve · Jun 23, 11:35 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48643426)

**Background**: Traditional OCR models use a KV cache that grows linearly with input length, causing out-of-memory errors on long documents. Developers often resort to splitting documents into pages, which adds complexity and breaks context. Recurrent sliding-window attention (R-SWA) is a novel architectural hack that reuses a fixed-size cache by sliding a window over the input, avoiding memory growth.

## [Nature Retracts Paper on Sub-second FRB Periodicity](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10799-8) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Nature has retracted a 2022 paper that claimed to detect sub-second periodicity in fast radio burst FRB 20191221A, because the periodic signal was actually from the known Galactic pulsar PSR J0248+6021. This retraction corrects a significant error in the astrophysics literature, reinforcing the importance of rigorous data analysis in high-impact discoveries. It also highlights the challenges in distinguishing fast radio bursts from pulsar signals. The original paper, published in Nature on July 13, 2022, analyzed the pulse profile of FRB 20191221A and reported sub-second periodicity, which was interpreted as evidence for a neutron-star origin. The retraction note states that the periodic signal was actually from the Galactic pulsar PSR J0248+6021.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 23, 00:00

**Background**: Fast radio bursts (FRBs) are intense, millisecond-duration radio pulses from extragalactic sources, whose origins remain largely unknown. Detecting periodicity in FRBs can provide crucial clues about their progenitors, such as neutron stars or magnetars. Pulsars are rotating neutron stars that emit regular radio pulses, and their signals can sometimes be confused with FRBs if not properly accounted for.

This analysis highlights a systemic flaw in peer review that can allow fraudulent research to slip through, undermining research integrity and public trust in science. The 'halo effect' refers to the tendency to give more favorable reviews to work from prestigious authors or institutions, which can be exploited by fraudsters. The article calls for reforms to reduce hierarchy-based bias in peer review.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 23, 00:00

**Background**: Peer review is a process where experts evaluate research before publication to ensure quality and validity. However, cognitive biases like the halo effect can distort this process, especially when reviewers are influenced by the reputation of authors or their institutions. Academic fraud, including fake data and fabricated studies, has become a growing concern, with high-profile retractions damaging trust in science.
