## [Ctenophore blastoporal organizer reveals signaling pathway origin](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10643-z) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Experiments on the ctenophore Mnemiopsis leidyi and the sea anemone Nematostella vectensis demonstrate that a blastoporal organizer, previously known only in bilaterians and cnidarians, also exists in ctenophores, suggesting that a core signaling pathway emerged early in animal evolution and enabled the transition to multicellularity. This finding provides experimental evidence that a key signaling pathway, likely Wnt or TGF-β, was a critical innovation for animal multicellularity, reshaping our understanding of early animal evolution and the origin of body axes. The study, published in Nature on June 17, 2026, used transplantation and molecular analyses to identify organizer activity at the blastopore of ctenophore embryos, similar to that in cnidarians, indicating an ancient origin of axial patterning mechanisms.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 17, 00:00

**Background**: The blastoporal organizer is a group of cells that directs the formation of the body axes during embryonic development, previously known in bilaterians (e.g., Spemann organizer in vertebrates) and cnidarians (e.g., sea anemones). Ctenophores (comb jellies) are among the earliest branching animal lineages, and their study helps reconstruct the ancestral state of animals. The emergence of intercellular signaling pathways like Wnt, Notch, and TGF-β is considered a hallmark of metazoan multicellularity.
