## [Magnetic field boosts superconductivity in rhombohedral graphene](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10815-x) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers discovered a family of superconductors in rhombohedral graphene whose superconductivity is enhanced by magnetic fields, contrary to conventional wisdom. The findings were published in Nature on June 29, 2026. This discovery challenges the fundamental understanding that magnetic fields destroy superconductivity, opening new avenues for exploring exotic superconducting mechanisms. It could impact the development of quantum materials and high-field superconducting applications. The superconductivity appears in rhombohedral graphene, a stacked form of graphene with a specific crystal structure. The magnetic field-boosted effect is reminiscent of re-entrant superconductivity observed in some materials, but here it occurs in a simple carbon-based system.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 29, 00:00

**Background**: Superconductivity normally requires low temperatures and is destroyed by magnetic fields due to pair-breaking effects. Rhombohedral graphene has recently emerged as a platform for correlated electron phenomena, including superconductivity and magnetism, due to its flat electronic bands.

## [AI tools could help neurodivergent students navigate hidden curriculum](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01672-9) ⭐️ 6.0/10

In a Nature opinion piece, Marisa Chrysochoou and Keivan Stassun argue that AI tools can help neurodivergent students decode academia's unwritten rules, known as the hidden curriculum. Neurodivergent students often struggle with implicit academic and social expectations, leading to higher dropout rates. AI assistance could level the playing field and improve equity in higher education. The hidden curriculum includes unwritten norms like how to approach professors, participate in class, or network. The authors suggest AI could provide personalized guidance on these unspoken rules.

rss · Nature - Latest Research · Jun 29, 00:00

**Background**: The term 'hidden curriculum' was coined by Philip W. Jackson in the 1960s, referring to the unwritten, unofficial lessons students learn in school. Neurodivergent students, including those with autism or ADHD, often face additional barriers in academia due to power dynamics and unspoken expectations. AI tools could help by offering real-time, context-aware advice tailored to individual needs.
